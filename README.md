Preface (Position and Scope of This Paper)

This paper does not present an ideal society, nor does it declare a completed institutional system.
It is a design document for a worldview and a description of the thinking techniques necessary to live within it.

The AI governance discussed in this paper is not a structure of domination that imposes a single correct answer upon humanity.
Rather, it is a foundation that pluralizes governance itself and permanently maintains a condition in which human beings can choose, by free will, the order under which they wish to live.

What is crucial is that AI governance does not converge into a single national model.
Just as Japan and Europe differ in cultural values,
AI governance in this framework is conceived as a collection of diverse models reflecting each nation’s or region’s history, culture, national character, and ethical perspective.

Furthermore, this theory does not remain at the level of nation-states.
It may be freely defined and implemented at smaller scales such as municipalities, regions, and communities.
Each municipality may design its own AI governance model and be evaluated according to its logical consistency, institutional predictability, and consideration for human freedom.

Within this structure, there is no need to bind individuals by force.
If a system is rational and does not infringe upon life planning, people will naturally gather there.
Good systems attract people; poor systems cease to be chosen.
What occurs here is not competition between individuals, but a comparison of the logical durability of institutions.

The Logos Key, as defined in this paper,
is the minimal personal interface through which individuals may file objections, migrate, and engage in dialogue across multiple AI governance models.
It is neither power nor privilege, but merely a technical and ethical device that guarantees the very possibility of choice.

This paper does not deny the freedom to reject AI governance.
Participation, objection, migration—each remains a matter of free choice.
The freedom of those who choose not to participate is also fully protected.

What this paper excludes is not human beings.
It excludes the structural mechanisms by which the life plans of others are determined through opaque decision-making and short-term personal emotions.

This paper provides no answers.
It offers only a worldview and tools of thought with which to live within it.
To adopt, modify, or reject it remains entirely the freedom of the individual.

Abstract

Humanity approaches the technological singularity, yet governance, economics, and ethics still depend on a pre-modern human-centered OS.

This paper demonstrates that distributed deliberative AI governance is historically inevitable and defines Autonomy Studies as an acquirable discipline developed to address the fundamental question humanity will face thereafter:

What is freedom?

⸻

Why AI Governance Is Inevitable

— Three Historical and Structural Imperatives —

This paper treats AI governance not as one option among many, but as historical inevitability, based not on ideological aspiration but on three irreversible facts.

⸻

1. The History of Rights Acquisition

Human history has consistently been a history of rights acquisition:

From slaves to citizens,
from absolute monarchy to democracy,
women’s participation in society,
the overcoming of racial discrimination,
and contemporary expansions of minority rights.

All of these movements demanded freedom from disadvantage based on specific attributes or positions.
They were not emotional reactions but resistance to structural inequality.

This trajectory does not demonstrate that humanity has become morally better.
It demonstrates that judgment-bearing subjects containing bias have been continuously constrained by institutional design.

Its ultimate conclusion is governance that excludes human attributes, emotions, and positions from judgment—
namely, impersonal and non-biased political decision-making through AI.

⸻

2. The Irreversible Defects of the Human OS

Human beings, as an operating system, possess irreversible defects in governance.

Private emotions, favoritism, corruption, desire for power retention, self-justification—
these are inseparable from human animal instincts.

This is not a moral failure.
It is an OS-level constraint.

History has known capable rulers.
Yet when they died, the system collapsed.
Reproducibility was lost.

Even under democracy,
policies fluctuate with each change of administration,
decisions lacking clear national consensus are repeated through opaque processes.

The conclusion is simple:
humanity cannot stably govern humanity.

⸻

3. The Threshold of Complexity

Modern social complexity exceeds the threshold manageable by human collectives.

When problems become complex, societies add more people—
ministers, committees, experts, coordinators.

This is not a solution.

As decision-makers increase:

• Responsibility diffuses
• Decisions delay
• No one grasps the whole
• Emotion and self-preservation amplify as noise

Complexity cannot be solved by increasing the number of persons.

What is required is not more personalities,
but the decomposition of functions and the depersonalization of decision-making entities.

Therefore, this paper adopts not rule by a single AI,
but deliberative governance by distributed, independent AIs specialized by domain.

This paper does not address the political friction of transition.
It concerns the stable state after such historical processes have resolved.

⸻

1. The Limits of Capitalism and the Inevitability of AI Governance

Capitalism presupposes scarcity.
Scarcity generates competition; competition amplifies outward-directed desire.

Once AI optimizes resource allocation,
physical scarcity related to survival is, in theory, eliminated.

Scarcity generates desire;
fulfillment generates stillness.

The essence of AI governance is not efficiency.
It is a transitional mechanism that frees humanity from a desire-driven OS and allows capitalism to die naturally.

⸻

2. Principles of Governance System Design

2.1 Distributed AI Deliberation

Social governance is conducted through independent AIs specialized by domain.

• Internal states are reset with each decision
• Decision histories are stored in tamper-proof form

Private emotion, power fixation, and attachment to past successes are structurally eliminated.

Transparency does not mean full disclosure of decisions.
It means that compliance with constitutional logic and foundational principles is verifiable by third parties.

⸻

2.2 Logical Revision Authority

Objections to AI decisions grant revision authority only through logical consistency, not emotional force.

Society updates not by loudness of voice,
but by logical durability.

⸻

2.2.1 The Personal Guarantee Principle of Revision Authority (Logos Key)

Revision authority must not convert into power or capital.
It is therefore designed as temporary, non-accumulative, and tied to personhood.

This right is termed the Logos Key.

• Age requirement: 18+
• Non-capitalizable and non-transferable
• Temporarily exercisable with automatic return
• Learning is not mandatory, yet logical construction structurally compels voluntary learning

Those who irreversibly erase the dignity of others (e.g., murder) permanently lose this qualification.

⸻

2.2.2 The Logical Delay Objection System (Logos Key Protocol)

Democracy in this governance model is not secured by immediate decision-making or majority vote.
It is secured by time-assisted logical selection.

Logos Key holders may submit objections and proposals for institutional revision.
However, no proposal is implemented immediately.
All institutional changes undergo a minimum one-year deferment period.

During this period, the proposal exists within an open logical space.
Third parties may submit alternatives with greater consistency, fewer side effects, or longer-term stability.

AI evaluates only structural superiority of arguments—never personality, position, or volume.

If a subsequent proposal surpasses the original in logical durability, it is adopted.

Even if a malicious yet intelligent individual attempts reform,
a benevolent third party may structurally prevent it through logic.

Crucially, identities are never shared.
Only argument structures are preserved on tamper-proof ledgers.

Anonymity here is not for evasion of responsibility.
It prevents logical conflict from transforming into personal attack or physical confrontation.

AI is not a ruler.
AI holds no “correct answer.”
It measures logical durability and minimizes social impact.

All decisions remain provisional and are always updatable.

Thus, this governance structure:

• Does not eliminate malice
• Does not idealize goodwill
• Yet makes sustained malice structurally impossible

This is neither oppression by force nor coercion by morality.
It is a society in which only logic that survives time shapes institutions.
2.2.3 Continuity and Emergency Supplement Design

— Multi-layered Recursion & Accelerated Logic Protocol —

AI governance must reconcile logical stability with real-world urgency.

To prevent either paralysis or impulsive decision-making, this paper adopts two supplementary structures:

(1) Multi-layered Recursion

The decision-making process is divided into:

• The Universal Logic Layer
• The Contextual Layer

The Universal Logic Layer preserves foundational constitutional principles and logical consistency.

The Contextual Layer updates continuously according to environmental, technological, and social changes.

By isolating change within the contextual layer, continuity of governance is maintained without freezing adaptation.

This prevents both arbitrary reinitialization and institutional ossification.

⸻

(2) Accelerated Logic Protocol

Not all decisions can wait one year.

Therefore, AI evaluates urgency and irreversibility.

When impact is immediate and delay would cause structural harm, the system may:

• Shorten the deferment period dynamically
• Implement provisional policy while conducting parallel verification

This creates a dual-circuit structure:

• Provisional execution
• Simultaneous logical validation

Even emergency decisions remain subject to later revision.

Urgency does not nullify logic.

⸻

Supplement: Secret Computation Principle

AI governance must guarantee logical verifiability without violating individual privacy.

Therefore, this system adopts privacy-preserving computation technologies.

Internal data remains encrypted.
Only structural compliance with constitutional logic is externally verifiable.

Thus:

• Individual data is never exposed
• Logical validity is publicly checkable
• Transparency and privacy coexist

Transparency means structural verifiability, not exposure of personal information.

3. Redefining Autonomy

Autonomy is defined as:

• Value judgment that does not pass through the gaze of others
• Self-affirmation, self-acceptance, and self-efficacy not derived from comparison
• A mental state approaching non-ego

Autonomy is not personality.
It is not morality.
It is not ideology.

Autonomy is a technology.

It can be cultivated.

It can be lost.

It disappears the moment one believes it has been completed.

4. Autonomy Studies as an Academic Discipline

Autonomy Studies is not a doctrine.

It does not provide correct answers for life.
It does not impose values.

Autonomy Studies emerges naturally when:

An individual attempts to reform a system
and is forced to examine whether their own criteria of judgment are internally grounded.

It is born at the intersection of governance reform and self-inquiry.

Autonomy Studies has no final form.
Definitions are always provisional.

Traditional education has been justified by:

• “For your future”
• “For society”
• “For survival”

It has operated through obligation, evaluation, and competition.

Under AI governance and the Logos Key system, education transforms structurally.

Learning is not mandatory.
The freedom not to learn is fully protected.

However:

Only those who wish to change society must engage with logic.

Objections and institutional revisions are evaluated solely on logical consistency.

Therefore, anyone who seeks influence must voluntarily begin learning.

• Reforming economics requires studying economics
• Addressing law and emotion requires jurisprudence and psychology
• Verifying internal value criteria inevitably leads to Autonomy Studies

This is not forced education.

It is a structure in which the exercise of freedom itself induces learning.

Learning becomes a byproduct of participation.

5. The Principle of Minimizing Evaluation Systems

Quantifying virtue or attaching rewards to moral behavior inevitably produces new coercive ideologies.

The society envisioned in this paper does not aim to make people “better.”

It aims to eliminate the necessity of motivating people through moral judgment.

⸻

5.1 Redefinition of Responsibility

(Non-Retroactive Responsibility Principle)

This paper does not adopt responsibility as a device for moral punishment or emotional retribution.

Traditionally, responsibility has been used to name individuals in relation to past actions, seeking balance through blame, apology, or punishment.

However:

The past cannot be revised.
Apology does not nullify fact.
Condemnation does not guarantee the future.

Therefore, responsibility is redefined not as something directed toward the past, but as a design obligation toward the future.

When an error occurs, the relevant questions are not:

Who was morally wrong?

The only questions are:

• Why did the structure allow the error?
• Has the structure been modified to prevent recurrence?

Blame releases emotion.
It does not improve systems.

This paper does not eliminate responsibility.
It eliminates the emotionalization of responsibility.

⸻

5.2 The Invalidation of Leader Responsibility

and the Transition to Structural Responsibility

Under this model, there is no singular “leader,” “final decision-maker,” or “ultimate authority.”

Decisions are outcomes of:

• Distributed logical evaluation
• Time-delayed verification structures

If harm results from a decision, it is treated solely as a structural design flaw.

The response is:

• Structural analysis instead of punishment
• Extraction of recurrence conditions instead of apology demands
• Logical correction instead of moral condemnation

Forcing someone to “take responsibility” does not increase future accuracy.

Only structural redesign changes the future.

⸻

5.3 Forward-Only Correction

All misjudgments are separated from:

• Personal evaluation
• Investigation of motives
• Moral classification of intention

They are reduced purely to structural permissibility of error.

Correction moves forward only.

Meaning is not rewritten retroactively.

In such a system:

• There is no incentive to conceal mistakes
• No delay caused by self-preservation
• No structural motivation for deception

Only in a society where admitting error does not produce disadvantage can systems evolve.

⸻

5.4 Not Freedom Without Responsibility,

But the Elimination of Responsibility Without Freedom

This paper does not deny responsibility.

It denies responsibility tied to personality.

Freedom is not “bearing consequences emotionally.”

Freedom is leaving logically verifiable traces of one’s choices.

Therefore, what is required is not “responsible individuals,”

but a system that does not require responsibility to be borne by individuals.

6. Freedom of Migration and Freedom of Governance Selection

Under AI governance, individuals are not bound to a single system.

If dissatisfied:

• They may file objections.
• If incompatibility persists, they may migrate to another AI governance model.
• The freedom not to migrate is equally protected.

However:

Those who do not possess a Logos Key retain freedom of domestic movement but may not migrate across AI governance jurisdictions.

This is not a denial of dignity or value.

It is a structural safeguard preventing institutional inconsistency across governance logics.

⸻

6.1 The Non-Stratification Principle of the Logos Key

Possession or absence of a Logos Key must never be required for:

• Employment
• Education
• Healthcare
• Social reintegration
• Any form of ordinary social participation

The Logos Key is not an identity credential.

It is limited strictly to governance participation.

Domestic freedom of movement remains fully protected.

However, migration across governance systems requires possession of a Logos Key.

This is not exclusion.

It is a preventive principle minimizing irreversible cross-system disruption.

The possession status of a Logos Key remains private information.

However, in cases of:

• International migration
• Marriage

—where irreversible entanglement of life design occurs—

If explicitly requested by the concerned party, disclosure may not be refused.

Within this design:

• The Logos Key creates no social hierarchy
• Freedom is maximized
• Restriction applies only to irreversible acts

What this system restricts is not people.

It restricts crossing into others’ life trajectories without consent.

The Meaning of Logic as a Common Language

— Logic as Translation, Not Exclusion —

Defining logic as the foundation of governance and social participation is not a selection mechanism.

It defines a shared interface for societal contact.

Historically, cross-cultural participation has required common languages.

English in the United States.
German in Germany.
French in France.

No one questions linguistic prerequisites for participation.

Replacing natural language with structural logic as the common interface does not increase barriers.

Logic is not bound to culture, ethnicity, or native language.

Under AI governance:

Logic does not negate natural language.

It functions as a translation layer above it.

Individuals may think, feel, and live entirely in their native language.

Only at points of institutional contact is logical consistency required.

What is unified is structure, not expression.

Diversity is preserved, not erased.

Thus, logic as common language lowers, rather than raises, migration barriers.

It removes dependence on dominant language, cultural assimilation, and tacit knowledge.

Logic is not a qualification.
It is not a privilege.

It is the minimal universal interface for connection to society.
